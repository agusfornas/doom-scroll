🏃 The Great Escape

Every Smart Person Just Left Big Tech And Got $1B Before Lunchtime

$18.8B in VC money chasing ex-Big Tech founders since 2025. David Silver: $1.1B seed. Yann LeCun: $1B for AMI Labs. This is a vibe shift.
April 30, 2026 · The Doom Scroll

CNBC published a piece this week that's basically a scorecard of everyone who left Big Tech to start an AI company and immediately raised obscene amounts of money. The number: $18.8 billion committed to AI startups founded since January 2025. Most of them are founded by people who were at Meta, Google, OpenAI, or Anthropic 18 months ago. The pattern: spend years at a big lab, get credentialed, watch your research area get deprioritized because it doesn't ship product fast enough, leave, raise $500M in four weeks, compete with your old employer.

The case studies are not subtle. David Silver, who co-created AlphaGo and legitimately helped invent modern AI as we know it, left DeepMind and closed $1.1 billion in seed funding for Ineffable Intelligence. Sequoia and Lightspeed led. The company was months old. Yann LeCun—who spent decades building Meta's AI research division and is considered a founding father of deep learning—left Meta and raised $1 billion for AMI Labs in March. Humans&, founded by ex-Anthropic and ex-xAI people, closed $480M in January. These are not small checks given to people with a pitch deck. These are commitments to individuals whose names alone carry research credibility.

The uncomfortable read here is what it says about Big Tech itself. The most credible AI researchers in the world are leaving, and they're leaving because the companies are prioritizing near-term revenue over fundamental research. That's a rational short-term business decision and a potentially catastrophic long-term one. The labs that win the next generation of AI capabilities may be the ones that gave researchers enough freedom to work on things that don't immediately ship. Meta and Google are betting they can acquire or outspend whoever figures it out. The researchers are betting the opposite. Someone's going to be very right about this.

🪦 Employment RIP

Meta and Microsoft Fired 20,000 People While Announcing Billions In AI Investment

The irony is structural and unavoidable. The AI that's worth $100B in infrastructure spend is also why 20,000 people don't have jobs this week.
April 30, 2026 · The Doom Scroll

Here's what happened this week in the corporate tech sector: Meta announced 8,000 layoffs. Microsoft issued mass voluntary buyout packages that analysts estimate will result in approximately 12,000 departures. Total: 20,000 people, in one week, from two of the most profitable technology companies on earth. The same week, both companies published earnings calls that emphasized record AI infrastructure investment. The juxtaposition wasn't even hidden. Meta literally said in its communications that it was "reorganizing for the AI era." The AI era, apparently, requires fewer humans.

The jobs being cut span a range: content moderation (automation), data analytics (automation), certain software engineering roles (automation), and general operational support functions (also automation). These aren't marginal roles. These are jobs that existed for a decade, that paid well, that built careers. They're being eliminated because AI systems have crossed the threshold of "good enough to replace this." Not "better than humans in every way"—just "better than humans enough that the ROI calculation has flipped." That threshold is moving fast and will keep moving.

The discourse online tends toward two camps: "this is normal economic churn, new jobs will be created" and "this is the beginning of structural unemployment at scale." The honest answer is: both can be true simultaneously at different time horizons, and that's cold comfort to the 20,000 people whose specific jobs in this specific moment were eliminated. The "new jobs will be created" argument requires years of labor market adjustment. The "you no longer have a job" reality is immediate. The timeline mismatch between disruption and adaptation is the actual crisis, not the long-run employment picture. No one lives in the long run.

🤖 Robots Getting Smarter

The Robots Working In BMW Factories Can Now Learn New Jobs On Their Own

Sereact's Cortex 2.0 raised $110M. 1B production picks. 1 error per 53,000. The robots are demonstrably more reliable than humans at this point and they're just getting started.
April 30, 2026 · The Doom Scroll

German startup Sereact raised $110M in Series B funding last week for their "AI robotic brain" platform, and the story is worth paying attention to beyond the funding number. Their product, Cortex 2.0, is a foundation model for industrial robotics that lets you redirect a robot to a completely new task by telling it what to do in natural language. No reprogramming. No new code. Just: "hey robot, we need you to do this now instead." The robot understands, reasons about the physical requirements, and starts doing the new thing. That's the product. BMW uses it. PepsiCo uses it. Daimler uses it.

The technical underpinning is what makes this more than a PR story. Cortex 2.0 combines a vision-language-action model with a world model—meaning the robot doesn't just understand instructions, it can simulate the physical consequences of its actions before executing them. It's the difference between a robot that follows a recipe and a robot that understands cooking. The reliability numbers are real: over one billion production picks completed, with human intervention required once every 53,000 requests. For context, human error rates in repetitive physical tasks tend to run significantly higher than that. On this specific metric, the robot is more reliable than the human it replaced.

The $110M goes to expanding into the US market with a Boston office, which means the next wave of this is coming to American manufacturing floors. The pitch to US manufacturers is simple: you have a labor shortage for skilled industrial work, and the people you can hire aren't as consistent as Cortex. That pitch will land. The doom framing isn't about the robots being evil—it's about the pace of this. Ten years ago, this was speculative robotics research. Five years ago, it was early deployment. Today it's Series B with BMW as a reference customer. In five more years, it'll be standard equipment in every major warehouse and assembly line. The adjustment window for affected workers is shorter than anyone is admitting publicly.
